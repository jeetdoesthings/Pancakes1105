1. Product Requirements Document (PRD)
1.1. Project Goal
To develop an AI-powered multi-agent system that automates the Request for Proposal 
(RFP) response process for Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs), enabling them to 
generate professional, strategically priced quotations in minutes rather than days. The 
system will integrate internal pricing, competitive analysis, and intelligent value-
diﬀerentiation to maximize win rates and proﬁtability.
1.2. Customer Persona: The Overwhelmed SME Sales Team
Our primary customer is the sales or business development team within an SME. They are 
typically lean, resource-constrained, and frequently face the challenge of responding to 
multiple RFPs under tight deadlines. They are losing deals to larger, faster competitors and 
struggle with inconsistent pricing strategies due to a lack of real-time market intelligence.
Key Pain Points:
•Time-Consuming Manual Process: RFPs require signiﬁcant manual eﬀort to gather 
information, analyze requirements, and draft responses.
•Lost Opportunities: Slow response times lead to losing bids to faster competitors.
•Suboptimal Pricing: Lack of competitive intelligence results in underpricing (eroding 
margins) or overpricing (losing bids).
•Resource Constraints: Limited staﬀ cannot handle multiple RFPs concurrently, 
creating bottlenecks.
•Inconsistent Quality: Manual processes are prone to errors and inconsistencies in 
proposals.

1.4. Winning Diﬀerentiators (The "X-Factors")
To stand out and win, our solution will go beyond basic functionality by focusing on these 
key diﬀerentiators:
1.4.1. Narrative-Driven Agent Roles
Instead of generic labels, our agents will have personas that reﬂect their specialized 
functions, making the system more intuitive and engaging for judges.
•"Junior Analyst" Agent: Responsible for parsing the unstructured RFP document, 
extracting key requirements, and structuring the data.
•"Pricing Strategist" Agent: Analyzes internal pricing, researches competitor data, and 
applies strategic pricing logic, including value-diﬀerentiation.
•"Senior Copywriter" Agent: Drafts the professional proposal content, incorporating all 
gathered information and strategic recommendations.
1.4.2. Intelligent Value-Diﬀerentiation Core Logic
This is the most critical diﬀerentiator, directly addressing the problem statement's core 
technical complexity.
•Logic: When the "Pricing Strategist" identiﬁes that a competitor's price is below the 
SME's base cost (making a direct price match unproﬁtable), it will autonomously pivot 
to a value-diﬀerentiation strategy. Instead of simply lowering the price, it will suggest 
adding non-monetary value-adds (e.g., extended warranty, premium support, 
expedited delivery, additional features) to the proposal.
•Explainability: The system will explicitly state why this strategy was chosen (e.g., 
"Competitor X is 15% cheaper, direct match is unproﬁtable. Recommending a 1-year 
extended warranty to diﬀerentiate.").

reasoning.

persuasive, and professional proposal text based on the structured data and strategic 
decisions from previous agents.

4.2. Real-World Data Strategy: Production Integration
In a real-world scenario, the SME02 system would need to integrate with various live data 
sources. This requires more robust and secure integration methods.
4.2.1. Internal Pricing & Product Data
Source: Typically resides in an SME's Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system, Product 
Information Management (PIM) system, or a dedicated pricing database.
Integration Methods:
•API Integration: The most common and preferred method. Many modern ERPs (e.g., 
SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics, Zoho CRM/ERP) oﬀer RESTful APIs for programmatic 
access to product, inventory, and pricing data. This allows real-time data retrieval.
•Database Connectors: Direct connection to the database (e.g., SQL, NoSQL) where 
pricing data is stored. This requires careful security considerations and database 
expertise.
•Data Warehousing/Lakes: For larger SMEs, data might be consolidated in a data 
warehouse or data lake, which can then be queried via APIs or direct connections.
•File-Based Sync: Less ideal but sometimes necessary for legacy systems. Regular 
exports (CSV, XML) from the ERP system that are then ingested by the SME02 system.
Considerations:
•Authentication & Authorization: Secure access using API keys, OAuth, or other 
enterprise authentication mechanisms.
•Data Freshness: Ensuring the pricing data is always up-to-date (real-time vs. scheduled 
sync).
•Data Transformation: Mapping internal data schemas to the format required by the 
SME02 system.
--- Page 11 ---
•Error Handling: Robust mechanisms for handling API failures, network issues, and data 
inconsistencies.
4.2.2. Competitor Data & Market Intelligence
Source: This is more complex as competitor data is often not publicly available in 
structured formats. It typically comes from a combination of sources:
•Market Intelligence Platforms: Subscription-based services that aggregate competitor 
pricing, product features, and market trends (e.g., Gartner, Forrester, specialized 
industry reports).
•Web Scraping (Ethical & Legal): Programmatically collecting publicly available pricing 
information from competitor websites. This must be done ethically, respecting 
robots.txt and terms of service, and is prone to breaking due to website changes.
•Public APIs: Some industries have public APIs that provide aggregated market data or 
pricing benchmarks.
•Sales Team Feedback: Qualitative data collected by the sales team during competitive 
bids, which can be structured and fed into the system.
•Third-Party Data Providers: Companies specializing in providing competitive pricing 
data for speciﬁc markets.
Integration Methods:
•API Integration: For market intelligence platforms or third-party data providers that 
oﬀer APIs.
•Custom Web Scrapers: Developed and maintained in-house for speciﬁc competitor 
websites.
•Manual Input/Review: For qualitative data or data from non-API sources, a human-in-
the-loop process for data entry and validation would be necessary.
Considerations:
•Legality & Ethics: Ensure all data collection methods comply with legal regulations 
(e.g., GDPR, CCPA) and ethical guidelines.
•Data Volatility: Competitor pricing can change frequently, requiring continuous 
monitoring and updates.
•Data Quality & Accuracy: Validating the accuracy of collected competitor data is 
crucial.
•Feature Matching: Accurately mapping competitor products/services to internal 
oﬀerings for a fair comparison.

4.2.3. Multi-Currency and Tax Handling
Source: External APIs for real-time exchange rates and internal tax databases/rules.
Integration Methods:
•Currency Exchange Rate APIs: Integrate with reliable APIs (e.g., ExchangeRate-API, 
Open Exchange Rates) to get real-time currency conversion rates.
•Tax Calculation Engines: Integrate with internal tax logic or third-party tax APIs (e.g., 
Avalara, TaxJar) to apply regional tax brackets correctly.
Considerations:
•API Rate Limits: Manage API calls to stay within limits.
•Compliance: Ensure tax calculations are compliant with local regulations.

5.3. Human-in-the-Loop Feedback and Modiﬁcation Workﬂow
One of the key diﬀerentiators of our system is its "Glass Box" UI and the ability for human 
users to intervene and guide the AI. This is crucial for maintaining control, ensuring 
accuracy, and incorporating nuanced business decisions that AI alone might miss.
5.3.1. The "Glass Box" UI for Real-time Monitoring
--- Page 16 ---
As the agents process the RFP, the UI will display a live feed of their actions and reasoning. 
This allows the user to:
•Monitor Progress: See which agent is currently active and what task it's performing.
•Understand Decisions: Observe the rationale behind pricing strategies, value-add 
recommendations, and content generation.
•Identify Potential Issues: Catch any misinterpretations or suboptimal decisions early 
in the process.
5.3.2. Interactive Approval and Modiﬁcation Points
The system will incorporate strategic pause points where human input can be provided. 
These are typically after a major agent completes its task or before a critical decision is 
ﬁnalized.
•Scenario 1: Modifying Extracted Requirements (After Junior Analyst)
•When: After the "Junior Analyst" agent has parsed the RFP and extracted key 
requirements into a structured format (e.g., JSON).
•How: The UI will present the extracted data in an editable format. The user can:
•Correct Errors: If the AI misidentiﬁed a quantity or a speciﬁc requirement, the 
user can directly edit the structured data.
•Add Missing Information: If the RFP was ambiguous or contained implicit 
requirements, the user can add them.
•Provide Speciﬁc Instructions: For example, "Ignore the budget constraint for 
now; prioritize premium hardware." or "Focus on upselling our extended 
warranty package."
•Impact: This ensures the subsequent agents (Pricing Strategist, Senior Copywriter) 
work with accurate and human-validated data.
•Scenario 2: Adjusting Pricing Strategy (After Pricing Strategist)
•When: After the "Pricing Strategist" agent has analyzed internal costs, competitor 
data, and proposed a pricing strategy (including value-diﬀerentiation).
•How: The UI will display:
•The proposed pricing breakdown.
•The agent's rationale for its pricing decision (e.g., "Recommended Premium 
Support Package due to competitor X being 15% cheaper.").
•Alternative options considered by the AI (e.g., "Option B: Match competitor 
price, but proﬁt margin reduced by 10%.").

•User Actions: The user can:
•Approve: Accept the recommended strategy.
•Override: Choose an alternative strategy (e.g., "Despite the lower margin,

•Reﬁne Value-Adds: "Instead of a 1-year warranty, oﬀer 6 months of free 
consulting."
•Give Speciﬁc Instructions: "Increase the price of Item X by 5% to account for

•Impact: This allows the SME to maintain strategic control over pricing and

•Remove Sections: If certain sections are not relevant for a particular client.

5.3.3. Technical Implementation of Human-in-the-Loop
•Backend Pause Points: The Multi-Agent Orchestrator will be designed with explicit pause 
points after each major agent's execution. At these points, the backend will send the 
current state and agent's output to the frontend.
•Frontend Interaction: The frontend UI will render the agent's output in an editable 
format (e.g., text ﬁelds, dropdowns, checkboxes). It will also have buttons like

•Feedback Loop: When the user submits modiﬁcations, the frontend sends the updated
